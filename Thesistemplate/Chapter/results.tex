\section{SQL Injection}
\label{sec:results_sql}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Comparison: ML vs static analysis.}\\
  On the SQL injection benchmark, the ML detector correctly flags 100 vulnerable
  functions and produces 14 false positives (TP=100, FP=14, FN=5, TN=20; Precision=0.88,
  Recall=0.95, F1=0.91, Accuracy=0.29). The static SQL analyzer identifies 211 vulnerable
  functions with 35 false positives and no false negatives (TP=211, FP=35, FN=0, TN=173;
  Precision=0.86, Recall=1.00, F1=0.92, Accuracy=0.92). Static analysis therefore achieves
  perfect recall and slightly higher F1 at the cost of a small increase in false positives,
  while the ML detector still reaches strong performance and illustrates that sequence
  models can approximate, but not fully match, the coverage of a tailored rule- and
  AST-based SQL injection scanner on this dataset.

  \begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
      \textbf{Detector} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1 Score} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
      \hline
      ML SQL detector & 0.88 & 0.95 & 0.91 & 0.29 \\
      Static SQL analyzer & 0.86 & 1.00 & 0.92 & 0.92 \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{SQL injection detection performance: ML SQL detector versus the static SQL analyzer.}
    \label{tab:sql_ml_vs_static}
  \end{table}

  \item \textbf{Comparison: Semgrep vs ML.}\\
  On the same SQL injection benchmark, the Semgrep-based SQL detector identifies 211
  vulnerable functions with 68 false positives and no false negatives (TP=211, FP=68,
  FN=0, TN=140; Precision=0.76, Recall=1.00, F1=0.86, Accuracy=0.84). The ML SQL
  detector, in contrast, reports 100 true positives with 14 false positives and 5 false
  negatives (TP=100, FP=14, FN=5, TN=20; Precision=0.88, Recall=0.95, F1=0.91,
  Accuracy=0.29). The ML model therefore achieves higher precision and a higher F1
  Score but lower recall than Semgrep, and its overall accuracy is lower because the
  dataset contains many safe functions that Semgrep classifies correctly. In practice, this
  suggests that Semgrep favors coverage and the ML model favors precision, while the
  static analyzer (Section~\ref{sec:sql_ml_vs_static}) combines high recall with a balanced
  false-positive rate.

  \begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
      \textbf{Detector} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1 Score} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
      \hline
      Semgrep SQL detector & 0.76 & 1.00 & 0.86 & 0.84 \\
      ML SQL detector & 0.88 & 0.95 & 0.91 & 0.29 \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{SQL injection detection performance: Semgrep SQL detector versus the ML SQL detector.}
    \label{tab:sql_semgrep_vs_ml}
  \end{table}

  \item \textbf{Comparison: Semgrep vs static analysis.}\\
  Comparing Semgrep directly to the static SQL analyzer shows that both detectors achieve
  perfect recall on this benchmark, but with different false-positive rates. Semgrep
  reports 211 true positives and 68 false positives (TP=211, FP=68, FN=0, TN=140;
  Precision=0.76, Recall=1.00, F1=0.86, Accuracy=0.84), while the static analyzer reports
  the same 211 true positives with only 35 false positives (TP=211, FP=35, FN=0, TN=173;
  Precision=0.86, Recall=1.00, F1=0.92, Accuracy=0.92). Static analysis therefore matches
  Semgrep's coverage on SQL injection but produces substantially fewer false alarms,
  which improves developer trust and reduces manual triage effort.

  \begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
      \textbf{Detector} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1 Score} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
      \hline
      Semgrep SQL detector & 0.76 & 1.00 & 0.86 & 0.84 \\
      Static SQL analyzer & 0.86 & 1.00 & 0.92 & 0.92 \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{SQL injection detection performance: Semgrep SQL detector versus the static SQL analyzer.}
    \label{tab:sql_semgrep_vs_static}
  \end{table}
\end{enumerate}

\section{XSS}
\label{sec:results_xss}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Comparison: ML vs static analysis.}\\
  On the XSS benchmark, the ML detector correctly flags 25 vulnerable functions and
  produces 10 false positives and 4 false negatives (TP=25, FP=10, FN=4, TN=28;
  Precision=0.71, Recall=0.86, F1=0.78, Accuracy=0.66). The static XSS analyzer detects
  all 42 vulnerable functions with the same 10 false positives and no false negatives
  (TP=42, FP=10, FN=0, TN=28; Precision=0.81, Recall=1.00, F1=0.89, Accuracy=0.88).
  Static analysis therefore achieves higher recall and a better F1 Score while keeping
  precision in a similar range, meaning it covers more true XSS cases without introducing
  significantly more noise than the ML detector.

  \begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
      \textbf{Detector} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1 Score} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
      \hline
      ML XSS detector & 0.71 & 0.86 & 0.78 & 0.66 \\
      Static XSS analyzer & 0.81 & 1.00 & 0.89 & 0.88 \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{XSS detection performance: ML XSS detector versus the static XSS analyzer.}
    \label{tab:xss_ml_vs_static}
  \end{table}

  \item \textbf{Comparison: Semgrep vs ML.}\\
  When comparing Semgrep to the ML XSS detector, Semgrep reports 15 true positives and
  13 false positives (TP=15, FP=13, FN=27, TN=25; Precision=0.54, Recall=0.36, F1=0.43,
  Accuracy=0.50), whereas the ML detector reports 25 true positives with 10 false positives
  and 4 false negatives (TP=25, FP=10, FN=4, TN=28; Precision=0.71, Recall=0.86,
  F1=0.78, Accuracy=0.66). The ML model therefore achieves higher precision, much higher
  recall, and a stronger F1 Score than Semgrep on this dataset, at the cost of a moderate
  increase in false positives. This suggests that the trained ML model captures a broader
  range of XSS patterns than the Semgrep rule configuration used in this experiment.

  \begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
      \textbf{Detector} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1 Score} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
      \hline
      Semgrep XSS detector & 0.54 & 0.36 & 0.43 & 0.50 \\
      ML XSS detector & 0.71 & 0.86 & 0.78 & 0.66 \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{XSS detection performance: Semgrep XSS detector versus the ML XSS detector.}
    \label{tab:xss_semgrep_vs_ml}
  \end{table}

  \item \textbf{Comparison: Semgrep vs static analysis.}\\
  Comparing Semgrep directly to the static XSS analyzer highlights the advantage of the
  tailored static rules. Semgrep detects 15 vulnerable functions and produces 13 false
  positives (TP=15, FP=13, FN=27, TN=25; Precision=0.54, Recall=0.36, F1=0.43,
  Accuracy=0.50), while the static analyzer detects all 42 vulnerable functions with
  10 false positives and no false negatives (TP=42, FP=10, FN=0, TN=28; Precision=0.81,
  Recall=1.00, F1=0.89, Accuracy=0.88). Static analysis thus achieves both higher recall
  and higher precision on this benchmark, providing more complete coverage with fewer
  false alarms than the Semgrep configuration.

  \begin{table}[htbp]
    \centering
    \small
    \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
      \textbf{Detector} & \textbf{Precision} & \textbf{Recall} & \textbf{F1 Score} & \textbf{Accuracy} \\
      \hline
      Semgrep XSS detector & 0.54 & 0.36 & 0.43 & 0.50 \\
      Static XSS analyzer & 0.81 & 1.00 & 0.89 & 0.88 \\
    \end{tabular}
    \caption{XSS detection performance: Semgrep XSS detector versus the static XSS analyzer.}
    \label{tab:xss_semgrep_vs_static}
  \end{table}
\end{enumerate}
